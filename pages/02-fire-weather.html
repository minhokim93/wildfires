    Dynamic Evolution of Fire Weather Risk

    Wildfire risk is changing spatiotemporally on a global scale due to growing fire weather risk. Days when weather conditions exceed a high-danger threshold are quantified as "Synchronous Fire Weather" (SFW) days — and these are increasing globally.

    Background What is Synchronous Fire Weather?

    Synchronous Fire Weather (SFW) defines concurrent climate extremes or spatially compound events which can significantly intensify hazards and exacerbate impacts. SFW is measured as the average number of days per year where local weather conditions exceed a high-danger threshold.

    This threshold is derived from a 40-year baseline climatology of the Fire Weather Index (FWI) — a composite meteorological metric combining temperature, humidity, wind speed, and drought codes to estimate fire danger.

    Three severity levels are assessed: severe (FWI90), extreme (FWI95), and catastrophic (FWI99) conditions, based on the 90th, 95th, and 99th percentile of the 40-year baseline at each grid cell.

    "If a fire season is increasing and eventually overlapping, it will shrink the window of opportunity to help each other in terms of firefighting."

    — Cong Yin, Climate Scientist, University of California, Merced
    Method How SFW Days Are Computed

    For each grid cell, the historical FWI distribution is derived from a 40-year baseline. Each daily FWI value is compared against the baseline threshold (e.g., FWI90). Days exceeding the threshold are counted as SFW days, and averaged per year.

    A synchronicity ratio is further computed based on SFW per month per pyrome, using a "sync threshold" that determines the proportion of minimum SFW grid cells that must be active simultaneously for the event to be considered synchronous.

    Finding Warming & Drying Trends Promote Fire Risk

    Global annual average measurements show the escalation of fire weather risk accelerating from 2020 onwards. Vapor Pressure Deficit (VPD) demonstrates a statistically significant rising trend (+0.0013 kPa/yr), while the Standardized Precipitation-Evapotranspiration Index (SPEI) shows a sharp drying trend (slope: −0.0010/yr).

    These combined trends — hotter and drier — create progressively more dangerous conditions for ignition and fire spread on an annual basis.

    Finding Expected Fire Weather Risk (Ω)

    A new metric called Expected Fire Weather Risk (Ω) is computed as the conditional probability that fire occurs given SFW conditions. The global mean Ω = 0.086, meaning roughly 8.6% of SFW days see an actual fire occurrence at a given location.

    This metric is compared with high-resolution global population data (WorldPop) at four-year intervals to assess the progression of human exposure. Countries with high risk × high population density — such as South Sudan, Mozambique, and Central African Republic in Africa, and Cambodia and Myanmar in Asia — face disproportionate exposure.

    Mitigating Risk as Shared Responsibility

    Defensible spaces are confined to property boundaries — but fire isn't. We rethink defensible spaces as overlapping buffers and develop spatial responsibility networks that quantify who owes what to whom in wildfire risk mitigation.

    SRShared Responsibility

    Problem Fixed Boundaries Are Meaningless to Fire

    Defensible spaces are maintained to clear flammable fuels and reduce ignition probability. By definition, defensible space is constrained to a property parcel's boundaries. But for fire, these fixed boundaries are meaningless — ignitions in neighboring properties can place nearby homeowners at risk regardless of what they do on their own land.

    Wildfire risk mitigation must be considered as a multi-parcel assessment. However, homeowners may not recognize the spillover effects of unmitigated hazards, or understand the complex overlapping risk regions between neighbors.

    This project reframes risk as "responsibility" and develops new spatial metrics that measure each homeowner's responsibility to mitigate wildfire risk according to shared and owed regions.

    Method From Defensible Spaces to Responsibility Zones

    Three responsibility types are defined by the spatial geometry of overlapping defensible space buffers:

    PR
    Personal Responsibility
    The defensible space unique to a homeowner's property — not overlapping with any neighbors.
    SR
    Shared Responsibility
    Overlapping regions between neighbors — risk that both homeowners contribute to and must mitigate together.
    OR
    Owed Responsibility
    Overlap from a neighbor's defensible space encroaching onto another's — risk that one homeowner "owes" mitigation for.

    Metrics are calculated as the product of spatial overlap [ft²] and the fire spread risk (Rate of Spread, ft/min) within that region, derived from FLAMMAP fire behavior simulations. SR and OR are measured in ft³/min — analogous to water accumulating in a rain gauge over time.

    Method Responsibility Networks: Who, Where, With Whom?

    Rather than computing simple raster products per property, we build spatial directed networks of responsibility. Nodes represent each property (sized by PR). Directed edges represent SR or OR between adjacent neighbors — the direction denoting who owes mitigation to whom.

    This network representation captures the interconnectivity between neighbors: who needs to collaborate with whom, and where the highest-risk overlapping zones are located across the community.

    Results Network Dismantling & Mitigation Strategies

    To simulate neighborhood-scale mitigation, network edges are iteratively removed and the total SR (or OR) and number of disconnected sub-networks are monitored. Three strategies are compared:

    Random — edges selected randomly (simulates ad-hoc awareness). Localized — first edge random, then adjacent edges (simulates word-of-mouth). Targeted — edges selected by descending SR or OR value (simulates data-driven allocation).

    Targeted mitigation reduces community-wide SR and OR most rapidly in both networks, followed by localized and random. The SR network exhibits multiple sub-networks; the OR network exhibits hotspot parcels — each requiring different collaborative strategies.
